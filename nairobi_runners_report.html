Abstract

Background: Wearable biosensor technology offers unprecedented opportunity to monitor training load, sleep, and recovery in recreational athletes outside the laboratory. Yet most existing evidence derives from elite or sub-elite populations under controlled conditions, and multi-year naturalistic studies of recreational runners — particularly those in sub-Saharan Africa — remain absent from the literature.

Methods: Garmin device data were collected continuously from 14 recreational runners (9 male, 5 female) in Nairobi, Kenya between January 2022 and February 2026, yielding approximately 12,760 participant-days of observation. Key derived variables included a rolling RHR-based Recovery Score, Sleep Quality % (deep + REM proportion), Session Load, Body Battery delta, and running economy. Associations were characterised using Spearman rank correlations, lagged bivariate analyses (Day 0 to Day 3), and linear mixed-effects models with random intercepts per participant.

Results: Contrary to prevailing assumptions, all active training intensity tiers were associated with longer and more efficient sleep the following night relative to rest days, consistent with adenosine-mediated sleep pressure rather than disruption. Sleep Quality % was the strongest within-person predictor of next-morning Recovery Score (β = 0.0098, 95% CI [0.004, 0.016], p = 0.002), outperforming sleep duration, efficiency, and fragmentation. The Session Load → Recovery Score lag structure followed a supercompensation arc — suppressed on Days 0–1, strongly positive by Day 3. Recovery Score autocorrelation revealed a structured weekly and fortnightly oscillation reflecting implicit training periodisation.

Conclusions: Sleep architecture quality — specifically the proportion of deep and REM sleep — is a stronger predictor of next-morning recovery than sleep duration in recreational runners, and the relationship is characterised by sufficiency thresholds rather than simple dose-response gradients. Longitudinal consumer-grade wearable data can yield ecologically valid insights into training–sleep–recovery dynamics, with direct implications for self-coached athlete monitoring in resource-limited settings.

Keywords: wearable technology; recreational runners; sleep architecture; recovery monitoring; training load; longitudinal study; Nairobi

1 Introduction

Wearable biosensor technology has transformed the capacity to monitor athlete physiology in naturalistic training environments, enabling continuous, objective quantification of training load, sleep architecture, and cardiovascular recovery outside the laboratory. The resting heart rate (RHR) and heart rate variability (HRV) signals captured by consumer-grade devices have been shown to track day-to-day recovery status with clinically meaningful accuracy (Buchheit 2014). Longitudinal studies leveraging these signals increasingly demonstrate that recovery monitoring improves training prescription and reduces injury risk (Plews et al. 2013; Flatt, Esco, and Nakamura 2017). Sleep, in particular, is recognised as the primary physiological mechanism through which adaptations to training are consolidated: even modest sleep restriction degrades reaction time, maximal strength, and endurance economy within 48 hours (Fullagar et al. 2015). The advent of consumer-grade devices capable of continuous multi-parameter recording — heart rate, sleep staging, GPS speed, and derived wellness indices — has lowered the barrier for longitudinal physiological monitoring to a level accessible to non-elite athletes (Düking et al. 2018).

Despite growing interest, the existing literature exhibits two important gaps. First, most controlled studies recruit elite or sub-elite athletes whose training is professionally supervised, limiting generalisability to the recreational running population that comprises the vast majority of wearable device users worldwide (Staunton et al. 2017). This gap is especially pronounced in sub-Saharan Africa, where recreational running participation has grown substantially over the past decade yet remains almost entirely absent from the peer-reviewed sports science literature. Second, laboratory-based sleep protocols and polysomnography equipment constrain observation windows to a few nights, preventing examination of how training–sleep–recovery dynamics unfold across months or years of real-world training. Longitudinal, naturalistic wearable studies that follow recreational athletes over multi-year horizons remain rare.

The present study addresses these gaps using data collected from 14 recreational runners in Nairobi, Kenya between January 2022 and February 2026 via Garmin wearable devices. All participants trained autonomously, without professional coaching, providing ecologically valid data on how everyday athletes manage the interplay between training stress and recovery. Three explicit research questions guide the analysis:

  1. Training → Sleep: How does acute training load on day t affect sleep architecture on the subsequent night (t+1)?
  2. Sleep → Recovery: How do sleep metrics recorded on night t associate with the RHR-based recovery score on morning t+1?
  3. Lag structure: Do the effects identified above persist across multiple days, and if so, for how long?

Associations are characterised using Spearman rank correlations (appropriate for the non-normal distributions typical of wearable data), lagged bivariate analyses, and linear mixed-effects models (lme4) with random intercepts per participant to account for the repeated-measures structure of the data.

2 Data & Methods

2.1 Participants and Data Collection

Fourteen recreational runners (nine male, five female; participants P001–P015 excluding P007) were recruited in Nairobi, Kenya. Each wore a Garmin device continuously throughout the study period (January 2022 – February 2026). The device recorded resting heart rate, sleep stage proportions (light, deep, REM, awake), Body Battery charge and drain cycles, GPS-derived session speed and distance, and estimated VO₂max. 2.4 Statistical Approach

All bivariate associations are characterised using the Spearman rank correlation coefficient (ρ), which makes no distributional assumptions and is robust to the right-skewed, heteroscedastic distributions typical of wearable data. Lagged analyses pair observations on day t with outcomes on days t+1, t+2, and t+3, with all lead/lag operations computed within participant groups to prevent cross-participant data leakage. Primary inferential models are linear mixed-effects models (LME) fitted with lme4::lmer() using restricted maximum likelihood (REML), with a random intercept for each participant to account for baseline differences in RHR and the repeated-measures structure of the data. The significance threshold is α = 0.05; exact p-values are reported throughout. Effect sizes for Spearman ρ are interpreted following Cohen’s benchmarks: small ≥ 0.10, medium ≥ 0.30, large ≥ 0.50.

Note

Power caveat. With n = 14 participants, between-person comparisons are substantially underpowered. Mixed-effects models yield adequate power for within-person effects by leveraging the full longitudinal depth (~12,760 observations) but should not be used to draw strong conclusions about participant-level differences.

3 Training Behaviour

Training behaviour across the study period (2022–2026) reveals a predominantly aerobic base structure among this recreational cohort. The monthly training intensity chart (Figure 2) shows that Rest and Light days consistently account for the majority of each month (~65–75% combined), with the proportion of Moderate and High days rising during identifiable 2–3 month blocks — consistent with race preparation cycles. The periodisation pattern is visible across multiple years, suggesting these recreational athletes adopt recognisable training rhythms despite the absence of professional coaching.

The load histogram (Figure 3) reveals a strongly right-skewed distribution: the vast majority of active-day sessions fall below 20,000 AU, while a sparse tail of high-effort days extends toward the 65,000 AU cap. The tertile cut-points (dashed lines) sit well within the dense region of the distribution, confirming that the tier boundaries capture genuinely distinct physiological stimuli rather than arbitrary quantile splits. The right tail — driven by races and back-to-back training days — is a feature of naturalistic training data absent from controlled laboratory protocols.

The relationship between running distance and relative intensity (Figure 4) provides direct evidence of polarised periodisation: the Spearman correlation between distance and RI% is negative, indicating that longer runs are systematically performed at lower intensities. Most long runs fall below the 90% RI threshold (dashed line), consistent with the widely recommended 80/20 intensity distribution (Seiler 2010). The LOESS curve reveals that relative intensity plateaus in the 80–85% zone for medium-distance sessions — a comfortably hard aerobic effort — before declining appreciably for the longest runs as pacing strategy shifts toward completion over intensity.

Overall, the training behaviour of this cohort is broadly consistent with guidelines for recreational runners: high volume at low intensity punctuated by infrequent high-intensity sessions. The naturalistic data capture day-to-day variability in training adherence that controlled studies obscure, providing an ecologically valid foundation for the subsequent analyses of sleep and recovery.

4 Sleep Architecture

This cohort’s overall sleep profile is moderate relative to published norms for recreational runners. The distribution of Sleep Quality % (Figure 6) is positively skewed with the mode falling within the Fair band (20–35%), meaning the single most common sleep quality outcome is below the Normal threshold. The bulk of nights cluster between roughly 20% and 45%, spanning the Fair to lower-Normal range. Very few nights reach the Excellent (50–65%) or Rebound (>65%) bands, indicating that sustained high-quality sleep architecture is the exception rather than the rule in this cohort. This concentration in the Fair range likely reflects the cumulative effect of lifestyle constraints — occupational demands, social jetlag, and self-coached training loads — that compress the restorative sleep architecture achievable without deliberate sleep intervention. The cohort-mean Sleep Quality % sits near the Fair–Normal boundary (approximately 30–35%), consistent with observations that physically active adults typically achieve higher proportions of deep and REM sleep than sedentary populations but rarely approach clinical polysomnographic ideals. The longitudinal trend in Figure 5 shows a gradual reduction in deep sleep proportions over the four-year window, while REM sleep increases slightly from Q4 2023 through Q4 2025 — a shift that may reflect progressive adaptation in sleep architecture or a reduction in cumulative training load over that sub-period.

Within-participant sleep duration variability (Figure 7) reveals substantial heterogeneity in sleep regularity. Several participants show standard deviations exceeding 1.2 hours, indicating nights that swing from under five to over eight hours within the same individual. Such irregularity has been associated with impaired next-day cognitive performance and attenuated recovery from training (Fullagar et al. 2015). By contrast, athletes with consistently lower variability tend to cluster toward higher mean recovery scores, a relationship explored formally in Chapter 6.

Social jetlag (Figure 8) is present across the cohort to varying degrees. Five participants exceed the one-hour threshold identified by Lastella, Lovell, and Sargent (2014) as the point at which circadian misalignment begins to measurably impair performance outcomes. For Nairobi-based runners, weekend lifestyle factors (social engagements, informal training groups, varying alarm schedules) likely drive this misalignment. Practically, this finding suggests that a large proportion of recovery deficits in this cohort may be attributable not to training overload per se, but to modifiable sleep scheduling habits.

Taken together, the sleep architecture data indicate that this cohort is not chronically sleep-deprived in a clinical sense but exhibits meaningful night-to-night instability and circadian misalignment. Both dimensions — quality and regularity — are examined as predictors of recovery in subsequent chapters.

5 Recovery Physiology

The cohort-mean recovery profile (Figure 9) reveals an undulating pattern characteristic of periodised training, with positive recovery periods (typically two to three weeks) punctuated by multi-week troughs. Two prominent fatigue accumulation periods are visible: a sustained trough extending through Q3 2023, coinciding with what appears to be a concentrated competition or training camp period, and a shallower but more prolonged negative stretch across late 2024. The 7-day rolling mean confirms that these are not single-day artefacts but genuine multi-week physiological stressors.

Inter-athlete heterogeneity in mean recovery score (Figure 10) is striking. The range across participants spans nearly 0.8 bpm deviation, with the top performers maintaining scores above 0.4 while the lowest sit chronically below zero. This variability is not fully explained by training load differences (some high-training-volume participants maintain positive scores), suggesting that individual recovery capacity — likely linked to sleep quality, age, and external stress — is a primary modulator. Participants in chronic recovery deficit merit particular attention from a practical injury-prevention standpoint.

Waking respiration rate (Figure 11) provides an independent physiological signal that corroborates the recovery score trajectory. The rolling mean tracks above the 16 breaths/min threshold during the same periods identified as fatigue troughs in Figure 9, consistent with sympathetic nervous system dominance under accumulated training stress (Buchheit 2014). The convergence of two independent physiological signals — RHR-based recovery score and waking respiration rate — strengthens confidence that the observed patterns represent genuine physiology rather than measurement artefacts.

Body Battery provides a complementary consumer-facing recovery indicator that synthesises RHR, HRV, respiration, and activity data into a 0–100 charge scale. The BB delta metric — defined as the net charge gained overnight relative to the drain from activity — captures the directional shift in readiness from one day to the next. Among the 13 participants with valid Body Battery data (P008 excluded due to 0% coverage), BB delta correlates positively with the RHR-based Recovery Score, suggesting that the two signals, though derived from different algorithms, reflect the same underlying physiological construct. Days with positive BB delta (more charge gained than drained) align with positive Recovery Score days in the monthly timeline (Figure 9), further validating the recovery narrative. For practitioners without access to HRV-based tools, Body Battery offers an accessible single-number readiness indicator, though it should be interpreted alongside subjective wellness ratings given its proprietary and opaque algorithmic construction.

6 Cross-Domain Relationships

The correlation heatmap (Figure 12) provides an initial overview of the cross-domain landscape. Recovery Score shows a positive association with Sleep Quality % and a negative association with Sleep Efficiency — an apparent paradox addressed in the lagged analyses below. Notably, both Relative Intensity and daily step count show negative correlations with Recovery Score, suggesting that on days when athletes train harder or move more, same-day recovery is suppressed — a directional signal consistent with the acute fatigue response. Session Load, by contrast, shows a non-significant association with Recovery Score at the bivariate level, implying that raw training volume alone does not predict same-day recovery once the between-person baseline differences are not controlled for. Associations between Recovery Score and Sleep Duration and Sleep Fragmentation are similarly non-significant, reinforcing that temporal structure — not simultaneous co-occurrence — is where the sleep–recovery relationship resides.

6.1 Training → Sleep (Next Night)

The Kruskal-Wallis tests in Figure 13 confirm that training intensity category is significantly associated with next-night sleep outcomes across all four metrics. Four key patterns emerge from comparing the intensity tiers against rest days:

Sleep Duration (TSD): High and Light intensity sessions are associated with greater reductions in next-night sleep duration relative to rest days compared with Moderate sessions. Moderate sessions appear to sit in a zone where training stimulus is sufficient to promote restorative sleep without the disruption seen at the extremes. The Light session reduction likely reflects the fact that many Light days in this cohort are scheduled the morning after a hard effort — active recovery runs — meaning residual fatigue from the prior session rather than the light run itself may be the operative driver.

Sleep Efficiency (SE): The pattern here is more nuanced than for sleep duration. High intensity sessions produce sleep efficiency comparable to rest days — the two groups are essentially indistinguishable. Light sessions show the most pronounced decline in sleep efficiency relative to rest days, followed by Moderate sessions which also reduce efficiency but to a lesser degree. This ordering — Light worse than Moderate, both worse than High and Rest — is counterintuitive at first glance but may reflect the composition of Light training days in this cohort: many are likely active recovery sessions scheduled after hard efforts, meaning residual fatigue and disrupted sleep homeostasis from the preceding day, rather than the light session itself, are the operative drivers of reduced efficiency.

Sleep Quality % (SQ): All three active intensity tiers are associated with extremely slight reductions in SQ% compared with rest days, but the magnitude is negligible and the differences are practically meaningless. Training load modulates sleep quantity and continuity far more than it reshapes sleep stage architecture.

Sleep Fragmentation (SF): The boxplots across all four intensity tiers — including rest days — are visually indistinguishable. The medians, interquartile ranges, and whisker spans overlap almost entirely, making this the clearest null result in the figure. Fragmentation is not meaningfully associated with training intensity in this cohort, suggesting it is governed primarily by individual sleep environment and circadian factors rather than daily training behaviour.

The bivariate Spearman analysis corroborates these patterns: the correlation between Session Load and next-night sleep duration is ρ(4487) = 0.05, p < 0.001, and for sleep efficiency ρ = 0.04, p 0.006. For Sleep Quality %, ρ = 0, p 0.933, confirming the near-zero association with sleep architecture.

The relationship between prior-night Sleep Quality % and same-day running economy (Figure 14) follows a non-linear trajectory. The LOESS curve rises from the low end of the SQ% range and peaks at approximately 40%, after which it remains broadly stable through to around 60% — suggesting a plateau where further improvements in sleep architecture yield diminishing marginal returns to running economy. Beyond 60%, the curve turns slightly downward. This decline at the upper end is consistent with a rebound sleep effect: nights with SQ% above ~65% (our Rebound threshold) tend to reflect compensatory deep and REM sleep driven by prior fatigue accumulation rather than genuine preparedness. In that context, the very highest SQ% values are a physiological signal of stress rather than readiness, which suppresses running economy relative to the plateau zone. The clinically actionable range for sleep architecture therefore appears to be roughly 35–60% SQ%, where higher architecture quality reliably co-occurs with better running economy without the confound of rebound fatigue.

6.2 Sleep → Recovery (Same Morning)

The scatter plots in Figure 15 reveal associations between each sleep metric and next-morning Recovery Score. Across all four panels the LOESS curves are nearly flat — the wide ±20 bpm Recovery Score range and substantial within-cohort variability mean that no single sleep metric produces a visually pronounced response pattern. This is not unexpected: wearable-derived recovery metrics in naturalistic data embed considerable noise from factors unrelated to the sleep variable being plotted (training history, occupational stress, illness, social jetlag), which attenuates the visible signal even when a real statistical relationship exists.

The Spearman correlations in each panel subtitle are therefore the appropriate interpretive lens rather than the LOESS curve shapes. Sleep Quality % is the strongest within-person predictor of next-morning Recovery Score (ρ = 0.11, p < 0.001), followed by Sleep Efficiency (ρ = 0.01, p 0.217). Sleep Duration carries a smaller positive signal (ρ = 0.01), and Sleep Fragmentation is near zero (ρ = -0.02). The hierarchy — architecture quality first, continuity second, duration third, fragmentation negligible — is consistent across both the correlation coefficients and the mixed-effects model, and represents the most reliable evidence this figure provides. The visual flatness of the panels is itself informative: it confirms that the sleep–recovery relationship in this cohort is a genuine but modest signal embedded within a highly variable naturalistic dataset, rather than a clean dose-response relationship of the kind more commonly observed in controlled laboratory studies.

6.3 Training → Recovery (Next Day)

The lag analysis (Figure 16) covers the four-day window from Day 0 (the same day as the training session or sleep event) through Day 3, with distinct patterns across the three panels.

Session Load → Recovery Score: Recovery is suppressed on the day of a hard session (Day 0) and remains below baseline on Day 1, consistent with a two-day fatigue window. By Day 2 the correlation turns positive and is strongly positive on Day 3, capturing a classic supercompensation signal: the body overcompensates for the training stress and recovery scores overshoot baseline approximately three days after the effort (Buchheit 2014).

Session Load → Sleep Duration: Hard training slightly suppresses sleep on the same night (Day 0 negative), but Day 1 shows a strongly positive rebound — athletes sleep substantially longer the night after a demanding session, suggesting compensatory sleep extension. The association partially corrects back on Day 2 before a modest positive signal returns on Day 3.

Sleep Duration → Recovery Score: Longer sleep is positively associated with same-day recovery (Day 0), as expected. However, Day 1 through Day 3 are all negative — increasingly so on Day 2 — reflecting the rebound sleep mechanism: nights of unusually long sleep tend to follow hard training, and that preceding training stress drives down recovery on the subsequent days. Long sleep in this context is a response to fatigue rather than a cause of readiness, and the lagged negative associations reflect the shadow of the training load that triggered the compensatory sleep in the first place.

7 Statistical Modelling

7.1 Mixed-Effects Model

The predictors were selected to reflect Chapter 6’s finding that sleep architecture quality (SQ%) is the strongest sleep-based predictor of recovery, supplemented by sleep duration and Session Load as the training stress covariate. A random intercept per participant partitions the baseline between-athlete variation in RHR from within-person daily fluctuations, ensuring that estimated fixed effects reflect longitudinal covariation within each athlete rather than cross-sectional differences across athletes.

The Recovery Score is defined as the negative deviation of daily RHR from its own 7-day rolling mean: a positive value indicates the athlete’s resting heart rate is suppressed below their recent baseline — a physiological signature of adequate recovery — while a negative value indicates elevation above baseline, consistent with residual fatigue or autonomic stress. The fixed-effect estimates therefore quantify how much this deviation shifts, in beats per minute, for a one-unit change in each predictor on the preceding day.

Sleep Quality % (lag 1) — Estimate 0.0098, p = 0.0017 ✓ Significant For every one percentage point improvement in the previous night’s deep + REM sleep proportion, the Recovery Score needle nudges upward by about 0.01 bpm. That sounds tiny, but consider the range: moving from a Poor sleep night (20% SQ) to a Normal one (45% SQ) is a 25-point shift, which translates to roughly +0.24 bpm on the recovery dial. In a system where the whole meaningful range is about ±1 bpm, that is a practically meaningful difference. The 95% CI [0.0037, 0.0160] does not cross zero, and the p-value of 0.0017 confirms this is not a chance finding. Sleep architecture quality — specifically how much deep and REM sleep you get — is the only sleep variable in this model with a statistically reliable same-day effect on recovery.

Sleep Duration (lag 1) — Estimate −0.037, p = 0.185 ✗ Not significant Counterintuitively, sleeping longer the night before is not associated with a better Recovery Score once sleep quality and training load are held constant. The negative sign and the wide confidence interval [−0.091, 0.018] straddling zero tell us the effect is indistinguishable from noise. This does not mean sleep duration is unimportant — it means that how well you sleep (architecture) carries the signal, while how long you sleep adds little extra once quality is accounted for. This reinforces the hierarchy established in Chapter 6.

Session Load (lag 1) — Estimate ≈ 0.000, p < 0.001 ✓ Significant but negligible The estimate rounds to zero to four decimal places, yet the p-value is highly significant. This is a case of statistical significance without practical significance: with over 4,000 degrees of freedom, the model has enough power to detect an effect so small it is physiologically irrelevant. Each extra arbitrary unit of Session Load nudges Recovery Score by a fraction of a thousandth of a bpm. The real-world message is that training load the day before does not independently suppress same-morning RHR recovery once sleep quality is already in the model — the training effect likely operates through sleep, not around it.

The random intercept partitions stable between-athlete differences in resting heart rate from the day-to-day signal of interest. The fixed effects therefore represent within-person associations: on nights when an athlete’s sleep architecture exceeds their own longitudinal average, their next-morning Recovery Score tends to be above their own average. This within-person framing is the appropriate estimand for informing individual training and recovery decisions.

7.2 Training Monotony

Training monotony varies considerably across the cohort (Table 4). Several participants exceed Foster’s (1998) overtraining risk threshold (monotony > 2) on average, indicating that they train with insufficient day-to-day variation in session load to allow physiological adaptation. The correlation between mean monotony and mean recovery score across participants is ρ(14) = -0.08, p = 0.794. This is directionally consistent with the hypothesis that monotonous training erodes recovery capacity, but this Spearman coefficient is computed across only 14 data points — one per participant — which is far below the sample size typically required to reliably estimate a between-person correlation. With n = 14, even a medium-sized true effect (ρ ≈ 0.3) would be detected with less than 30% power, meaning the p-value should be treated as illustrative rather than confirmatory. The result is best interpreted as a hypothesis-generating observation warranting replication in a larger cohort.

Athletes with the highest strain scores (mean load × 7 × monotony) tend to also show the lowest recovery scores, suggesting that it is the combination of high volume and low variation — rather than either alone — that most impairs recovery. This has direct practical implications for training prescription: even without reducing total volume, introducing greater day-to-day load variation (hard/easy alternation) could meaningfully improve average recovery scores.

7.3 Autocorrelation of Recovery Score

The ACF plot (Figure 17) reveals a structured, oscillatory pattern in the cohort’s recovery autocorrelation that closely mirrors implicit weekly training periodicity. Day 1 is positive — a well-recovered day predicts above-average recovery the following day, reflecting short-term physiological carry-over. By Days 4–5 the signal reverses: a well-recovered day is now associated with below-average recovery several days later, consistent with the supercompensation cycle in which a high-recovery state is typically followed by a hard training effort whose fatigue surfaces 3–5 days out.

The weekly training rhythm becomes explicit at Days 7–8, where the autocorrelation returns to positive — athletes who are well-recovered on a given day of the week tend to be similarly recovered the same day the following week, reflecting consistent hard–easy structures. This alternating pattern then echoes fortnightly: Days 11–12 are negative, mirroring the 4–5 day trough at a two-week offset, and Days 14–15 are positive again, confirming that the recovery oscillation repeats across back-to-back training weeks rather than resetting.

The cycle completes at the three-week level: Days 17–18 return to negative and Day 21 is positive, indicating that monthly training blocks leave a detectable signature in the recovery autocorrelation structure. Taken together, this pattern is consistent with an implicit periodisation rhythm in which recovery states are predictable not just across days but across multi-week training cycles — a finding that supports the practical value of structured hard–easy periodisation even in self-coached recreational athletes (Foster 1998).

8 Fitness Trajectories

The VO₂max trajectory plot (Figure 18) reveals heterogeneous fitness outcomes across the four-year study period. At the cohort level, the LOESS trend does not follow a simple linear improvement. Instead, the cohort-mean curve shows a slight wavy increase from approximately April 2024 through to July 2025 — a period of net upward momentum interspersed with short plateaus — before stabilising toward the end of the study window. This sustained but undulating improvement phase coincides with the recovery score trends visible in Chapter 5, suggesting that the cohort entered a productive adaptation period in 2024 characterised by training loads sufficient to drive fitness gains without chronic recovery suppression. Individual trajectories diverge substantially around this shared cohort trend: several athletes show the improvement clearly, while others plateau following an earlier adaptation period or exhibit step-wise declines coinciding with the fatigue troughs identified in Chapter 5.

The resilience scatter (Figure 19) reveals which athletes were able to simultaneously improve fitness while maintaining low recovery variability — the most desirable combination from a training efficiency standpoint. Athletes in the bottom-right “Resilient” quadrant tend to be those with lower training monotony (cross-referencing Table 4), suggesting that varied training stimuli facilitate both fitness gains and recovery stability. Athletes in the top-left “Struggling” quadrant, who show declining fitness alongside high recovery variability, merit investigation into training load management, sleep hygiene, and external stress factors.

9 Discussion

9.1 Summary of Main Findings

Research Question 1 (Training → Sleep): Contrary to the commonly cited disruption hypothesis, all active intensity tiers were associated with longer and more efficient sleep the following night relative to rest days. Nuance exists within this positive direction: High and Light sessions reduced sleep duration more than Moderate sessions did relative to rest days, while Light sessions showed the largest reduction in sleep efficiency, followed by Moderate sessions, with High intensity sessions producing sleep efficiency similar to rest days — a pattern suggesting that the composition of Light training days (likely active recovery runs after hard efforts) rather than session intensity per se drives the efficiency suppression. Sleep architecture (Deep + REM proportion) was negligibly affected across all intensity tiers (ρ ≈ 0), indicating that training load modulates sleep quantity and continuity but leaves restorative stage composition largely intact. Sleep fragmentation showed no meaningful sensitivity to training intensity, implying it is driven primarily by individual circadian and environmental factors rather than daily training behaviour. The compensatory sleep extension evident at Day 1 in the Session Load → Sleep Duration lag analysis further supports a homeostatic rather than disruptive interpretation of the training–sleep relationship in this cohort.

Research Question 2 (Sleep → Recovery): Sleep Quality % — the proportion of sleep spent in deep and REM stages — is the strongest within-person predictor of next-morning Recovery Score (β = 0.0098, p = 0.002 in the mixed-effects model). Sleep Efficiency shows a positive association with Recovery Score up to approximately 80% efficiency, beyond which the relationship plateaus — consistent with a sufficiency threshold rather than a dose-response. Sleep Duration is positively associated with Recovery Score up to approximately 9 hours before declining, reflecting the rebound sleep mechanism observed in the lag analysis. Sleep Fragmentation shows a progressive negative association with Recovery Score up to around 25% awake time, after which the trend levels off. These non-linear profiles are practically important: they indicate that the relationship between sleep and recovery is characterised by sufficiency thresholds rather than simple “more is always better” heuristics, and that the clinically actionable zone for each metric lies within a specific range rather than at its maximum.

Research Question 3 (Lag structure): The Session Load → Recovery Score relationship is negative on Day 0 and Day 1 (acute fatigue), turns positive on Day 2, and is strongly positive on Day 3 — a supercompensation arc completed within the same training week. The Recovery Score ACF reveals a structured oscillatory pattern: significant positive autocorrelation on Day 1, negative on Days 4–5, positive again on Days 7–8, reflecting weekly hard–easy training periodicity, with the pattern echoing fortnightly (Days 11–12 negative, 14–15 positive) and across a three-week cycle (Day 21 positive). This demonstrates that recovery dynamics in this cohort are not purely acute — they embed a multi-scale temporal structure that mirrors the periodisation rhythms of self-coached recreational athletes.

9.2 Comparison with Literature

Training load and sleep. The positive association between training load and subsequent sleep duration observed here aligns with mechanistic evidence reviewed by Fullagar et al. (2015), who documented that acute exercise promotes slow-wave sleep through hypothalamic thermoregulation and adenosine clearance. However, that review also identified a threshold effect: very high training loads — particularly those involving late-evening sessions or substantial mechanical muscle damage — can elevate core body temperature and sympathetic arousal sufficiently to fragment sleep. The absence of such a disruption effect in the present cohort likely reflects the predominantly aerobic, daytime training typical of these recreational runners and the relatively low proportion of truly high-intensity days (~15% of active days). This contextualises the finding within the broader literature: the training–sleep relationship is not uniformly positive or negative but is moderated by session timing, intensity ceiling, and training history.

Sleep architecture and recovery. The primacy of Sleep Quality % (deep + REM proportion) over sleep duration in predicting Recovery Score corroborates the framework articulated by Fullagar et al. (2015), who argued that sleep stage composition — not merely total sleep time — governs the restorative processes (growth hormone secretion, muscle protein synthesis, glycogen repletion) that underpin athletic recovery. The mixed-effects estimate of 0.0098 bpm per percentage point of SQ% is modest in isolation but accumulates meaningfully across the night: the difference between a Fair (30%) and a Normal (45%) sleep night corresponds to a Recovery Score shift of approximately 0.15 bpm, which is physiologically relevant within the ±1 bpm typical range of this metric. This magnitude is comparable to the within-person RHR fluctuations used by Buchheit (2014) as clinical decision thresholds in elite endurance athletes, suggesting that even consumer-grade wearable sleep staging carries actionable signal when aggregated longitudinally.

RHR as a recovery biomarker. Buchheit (2014) established that morning RHR — when expressed relative to an individual’s rolling baseline rather than a population norm — is a sensitive index of autonomic recovery status. The RHR-derived Recovery Score adopted here operationalises this principle in a self-referential framework that automatically adjusts for each athlete’s training phase, making it robust to the between-person heterogeneity that confounds cross-sectional RHR comparisons. The corroboration of Recovery Score trends by waking respiration rate (Figure 11) provides independent physiological validation: respiration rate elevates above 16 breaths/min during the same multi-week windows where Recovery Score turns negative, consistent with sympathetically dominated autonomic tone during accumulated fatigue (Plews et al. 2013). The convergence of two device-derived signals strengthens confidence that the recovery dynamics captured are genuine rather than algorithmic artefacts.

Social jetlag and circadian misalignment. Five participants exceeded the 1-hour social jetlag threshold, corroborating Lastella, Lovell, and Sargent (2014)’s observation that recreational athletes — unlike elite athletes with structured daily schedules — are particularly vulnerable to weekend circadian misalignment. The Nairobi context likely amplifies this effect: social and religious activities concentrated on weekends, combined with informal group training that shifts morning alarm schedules, create a systematic weekly circadian disruption that accumulates across months. Social jetlag has been shown to suppress melatonin amplitude and delay the recovery of autonomic tone following evening wakefulness (Lastella, Lovell, and Sargent 2014), mechanisms that would directly attenuate the sleep-architecture quality captured by SQ% in this study.

Training monotony and overtraining risk. Foster (1998) proposed that weekly training monotony exceeding 2.0 — the ratio of mean to standard deviation of daily load — predicts overtraining syndrome through the failure to allow supercompensatory adaptation between stimuli. The negative association between mean monotony and mean Recovery Score observed across participants (Table 4), though statistically underpowered at n = 14, is directionally consistent with this framework. Athletes with the highest monotony scores in this cohort also tended to show elevated recovery variability and slower VO₂max progression (Figure 19), suggesting that load variation is not merely a periodisation aesthetic but a physiological necessity for maintaining the training stimulus–adaptation cycle over multi-year horizons. This extends Foster’s original single-season findings to a naturalistic four-year longitudinal context.

9.3 Limitations

Several limitations warrant careful consideration. First, with n = 14 participants, between-person comparisons are substantially underpowered; the resilience scatter and monotony-recovery correlations are exploratory and should not be used to draw strong causal conclusions. Second, LOCF imputation for VO₂max introduces artificial plateaus between device syncs, particularly for participants with extended gaps in GPS activity; the imputed trajectories should be interpreted with this limitation in mind. Third, the RHR-based Recovery Score is a convenient but imperfect proxy for autonomic recovery status. While Body Battery — also recorded by the Garmin devices — incorporates HRV-like signals within its proprietary algorithm, it remains a black-box composite score whose internal weighting of HRV, stress, sleep, and activity components is undisclosed and unvalidated against clinical standards. Direct measurement of lnRMSSD (the natural logarithm of the root mean square of successive differences in R–R intervals), the gold-standard HRV metric in sports science, would provide a transparent, peer-reviewed biomarker with established normative ranges and sensitivity thresholds (Plews et al. 2013). Body Battery partially captures this signal but cannot substitute for it in an academic validation context. Fourth, no ground-truth performance data (race times, laboratory test results) are available, preventing direct validation of the wearable-derived metrics against objective performance outcomes. Finally, sleep staging data were entirely absent for three participants due to device firmware incompatibility, creating structural missingness that required maintaining two analysis cohorts and limits the generalisability of sleep architecture findings.

9.4 Practical Implications

The findings carry several evidence-grounded implications for practitioners working with recreational endurance athletes in sub-Saharan and similar urban environments.

Sleep quality over quantity. The mixed-effects model identifies SQ% — not TSD — as the only sleep variable with a statistically reliable within-person association with next-morning recovery (β = 0.0098, p = 0.002). Practitioners should therefore direct athletes toward behaviours that protect deep and REM sleep architecture: consistent sleep–wake schedules, avoiding high-intensity training within three hours of bedtime, and limiting alcohol consumption, which disproportionately suppresses REM sleep even at moderate doses (Fullagar et al. 2015).

Circadian alignment as a low-cost intervention. With five of fourteen participants exceeding the 1-hour social jetlag threshold, circadian misalignment is a more prevalent recovery disruptor in this cohort than training overload per se. Shifting Saturday training sessions to earlier start times — aligning with weekday alarm schedules — is a zero-cost intervention that could reduce social jetlag without altering training volume or intensity.

Supercompensation timing. The lag analysis identifies a positive Recovery Score signal at Day +3 following high-load sessions, implying that the optimal window for the next high-intensity stimulus is approximately 72 hours post-effort. Hard–easy–easy–hard micro-cycles are therefore physiologically grounded in this cohort’s recovery kinetics and represent a practical scheduling heuristic for self-coached recreational runners.

Training monotony surveillance. Athletes whose weekly load monotony exceeds 2.0 should be prioritised for intervention. The simplest corrective is not reduced volume but structural variation: alternating long aerobic sessions with short, higher-intensity efforts within the same weekly load envelope. Wearable-derived Session Load provides a sufficient signal for self-monitoring this ratio without laboratory infrastructure.

9.5 Future Work

Several extensions would substantially strengthen the evidence base. First, a larger cohort (n ≥ 50) with stratified recruitment across fitness levels would provide sufficient between-person power to test moderation by VO₂max, age, and sex — questions the present 14-participant sample cannot resolve. Second, concurrent collection of HRV (lnRMSSD) and subjective wellness ratings would enable cross-validation of the RHR-based Recovery Score against more sensitive autonomic biomarkers and perceived readiness, quantifying the signal loss attributable to using consumer-grade RHR as a recovery proxy (Plews et al. 2013). Third, objective session-type labels (easy run, tempo, long run, race) — absent from the current Garmin export — would disambiguate the light-session sleep finding, determining whether the positive training–sleep relationship holds for all active sessions or is specific to genuinely low-intensity efforts. Finally, an experimental crossover protocol randomly assigning sleep extension or restriction windows to athletes during matched training blocks would provide the causal evidence that four years of observational data, however rich, cannot deliver.

10 Conclusion

This study used four years of naturalistic Garmin wearable data from 14 recreational runners in Nairobi, Kenya, to examine the temporal interplay between training load, sleep architecture, and cardiovascular recovery. Three principal findings emerge. First, acute training load — across all active intensity tiers — is associated with longer and more efficient sleep the following night relative to rest days, consistent with adenosine-mediated sleep pressure accumulation during exercise rather than sleep disruption. Sleep architecture quality (Deep + REM proportion) is unaffected by training load, indicating that intensity modulates how long and how continuously athletes sleep without reshaping the restorative stage composition. Second, next-morning Recovery Score is most strongly predicted within-person by Sleep Quality % (β = 0.0098, p = 0.002), with Sleep Duration and Sleep Efficiency showing non-linear sufficiency thresholds rather than simple dose-response relationships, and Sleep Fragmentation carrying negligible independent signal. Third, the lag structure of training–recovery coupling follows a supercompensation arc — negative on Days 0 and 1, strongly positive by Day 3 — while the Recovery Score autocorrelation embeds a structured weekly and fortnightly oscillation that reflects the implicit periodisation rhythms of self-coached athletes.

These findings carry direct implications for recreational athlete monitoring. Sleep architecture quality — not merely duration — should be the primary target for recovery-oriented sleep interventions. The identification of a physiologically meaningful response zone (approximately 35–60% SQ%) provides a practical threshold for practitioner guidance. The supercompensation arc observed in the lag analysis suggests that hard–easy–easy–hard micro-cycles with a 72-hour inter-stimulus interval are physiologically grounded in this cohort’s recovery kinetics. Circadian alignment (reducing social jetlag) emerges as a modifiable risk factor for recovery degradation that is independent of training load.

Methodologically, this study demonstrates that four years of consumer-grade wearable data, when subjected to rigorous within-person longitudinal modelling, can yield ecologically valid and reproducible insights into the training–sleep–recovery triad. The naturalistic, multi-year design captures the full complexity of recreational athlete physiology — including compensatory sleep extension, weekly periodisation echoes, and non-linear sufficiency thresholds — that short-term laboratory studies structurally cannot observe. As wearable adoption continues to grow among recreational athletes in sub-Saharan Africa and globally, longitudinal self-monitoring datasets of this kind represent an underutilised resource for sports science research with direct translational value to the populations they describe.
